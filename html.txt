<section id="intro">
<h2>Introduction</h2>

<div class="box">
South Korea's shipbuilding industry is a global leader known for building complex,
high-value vessels — including LNG carriers, ultra-large container ships,
offshore platforms, and increasingly green and autonomous ships.
</div>

<p>
Its success rests on deep engineering capabilities, clustered supply chains,
and continuous investment in research and development.
</p>

<p>
This page outlines the industry's background and practical ways Korean shipbuilding
can support MASGA (Make American Shipbuilding Great Again).
</p>

</section>



<section id="background">

<h2>Background</h2>

<p>
Korea's shipbuilding rise began under strategic industrial policies and investments
in the 1970s–1990s. Concentrated shipbuilding hubs such as Ulsan, Geoje,
and Mokpo enabled economies of scale and a dense supplier ecosystem.
</p>

<p>
Large private shipyards, supported by coordinated public initiatives,
focused on high-margin, technology-intensive ships rather than low-cost commodity vessels.
</p>


<ul>
<li>Industrial clusters connecting yards, suppliers, and research institutions</li>
<li>Heavy investment in digital design, modular construction, and green propulsion</li>
<li>Strong export orientation based on long-term contracts with global shipowners</li>
<li>Integrated supplier networks reducing lead times and controlling costs</li>
</ul>

</section>



<section id="help">

<h2>How the Korean Shipbuilding Industry Can Help MASGA</h2>

<p>
MASGA aims to restore American shipbuilding strength. Korea offers both a strategic
model and concrete capabilities that can support rebuilding domestic capacity
through international partnership.
</p>


<div class="card">
<h3>1. Strategic Technology Partnerships</h3>
<p>
Establish bilateral R&D programs and joint ventures with Korean yards to transfer
advanced hull designs, LNG and alternative-fuel propulsion systems,
and digital shipbuilding technologies.
</p>
</div>


<div class="card">
<h3>2. Modular Construction & Productivity Programs</h3>
<p>
Pilot modular block construction projects in U.S. yards under Korean technical guidance.
Train-the-trainer programs can institutionalize methods that reduce assembly time
and defects while increasing productivity.
</p>
</div>


<div class="card">
<h3>3. Supplier Development & Joint Procurement</h3>
<p>
Use Korean experience to identify supplier gaps and develop incentives
to grow a U.S.-based marine components industry.
</p>
</div>


<div class="card">
<h3>4. Workforce Exchange & Training Hubs</h3>
<p>
Create exchange programs between Korean vocational institutes and U.S.
technical colleges to transfer productivity techniques and training models.
</p>
</div>


<div class="card">
<h3>5. Green Fleet Transition</h3>
<p>
Coordinate on LNG, methanol, ammonia-ready, hybrid-electric, and other
low-emission vessel technologies to create future opportunities.
</p>
</div>


<div class="card">
<h3>6. Policy & Financing Instruments</h3>
<p>
Adopt Korea-inspired industrial tools adapted for the U.S. context:
long-term financing, export credit support, cluster-friendly zoning,
and procurement guarantees.
</p>
</div>


<div class="box">
<strong>Implementation Note:</strong><br>
Partnerships should protect U.S. industrial base priorities through
intellectual property rules, phased localization targets, and workforce commitments
while enabling rapid capability transfer.
</div>

</section>
